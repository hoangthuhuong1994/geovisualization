Soil Erosion in West-Kenya

Introduction

The tasks of this project is to calculate the Revised Universal Soil Loss Equation (RUSLE) to estimates the average annual soil erosion in West-Kenya.

The RUSLE equation is as follows: A = R x K x LS x C x P, where:

  • A: average annual soil erosion/li>
  • R: Effect of precipitation
  • K: Soil effect
  • LS: Terrain properties (length&slope)
  • C: effects of plants, soil cover (crop)
  • P: Measures to protect against erosion

Map of the Study Area

To acquaint with the study area in West-Kenya, I created a map using tiles from a global dataset to construct a DEM. These tiles were merged into a unified raster layer and reprojected into a coordinate system appropriate for the region. The map highlights major cities, rivers and water reservoirs. The projection of the map used to create the map is WGS_1984_Lambert_Azimuthal_Equal_Area.

Calculating the LS Factor

In the RUSLE framework, the LS-factor, which combines Length (L) and Slope (S) values, is crucial for assessing soil erosion risk. Higher L and S values indicate a greater potential for soil loss. The LS-factor is computed by multiplying corresponding pixel values of slope area by slope steepness which allows for soil erosion risk assessment on a per-pixel basis. Calculating the C Factor

The C factor in soil erosion models quantifies the effect of crop cover and management practices on soil erosion rates. It is pivotal in understanding how vegetation protects soil from erosion.

The Normalized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) is a satellite-derived measure to estimate vegetation health and coverage. NDVI values range from -1 (non-vegetated areas) to +1 (dense vegetation). The relationship between NDVI values and the C factor is modelled using nonlinear (exponential) regression, indicating how vegetation coverage (or lack thereof) affects erosion risk.

Therefore, in areas where NDVI values are high (close to 1), indicating dense and healthy vegetation, the corresponding C factor equation would generally be low (close to 0), reflecting the high level of protection against erosion provided by the vegetation cover.

Calculating the K Factor

K-factor in soil erosion studies represents soil erodibility, indicating how susceptible soil is to erosion and the rate of runoff, largely influenced by soil texture. The K-factor is calculated using a formula that incorporates the soil's textural factors, including percentages of silt, very fine sand and clay, as well as organic matter content, soil structure class, and permeability class. K-factor has values > 0, normally < 0.4, for soils with a moderate fraction of silt*, values are in [t·h·MJ−1·cm−1].

Calculating the R Factor

The R factor is known as the rainfall erosivity factor quantifies the impact of rain's kinetic energy and its capacity to cause soil erosion. It is mainly focused on the sum of energy from extreme rain events with a maximum duration of 30 minutes, averaged annually.

Analyzing the result by doing a look up for values from RUSLE and all sub-factors:

Case A, conditions: Gentle slopes (Low LS), intense rainfall (High R), dense vegetation cover (Low C), and soil with low erodibility (Low K). The adverse effects of high rainfall erosivity are counterbalanced by the protective influences of gentle slopes, dense vegetation, and low soil erodibility. Therefore, despite rainfall's high erosivity potential, the erosion risk remains low to moderate, highlighting the importance of vegetation cover and inherent soil properties in erosion mitigation.

Case B, conditions: Gentle slopes (Low LS) with heavy rainfall (High R) on with good vegetation cover (Low C) but on highly erodible soil (High K). This leads to potential soil erosion risk due to soil erodibility, mitigated by vegetation and conservation practices.
